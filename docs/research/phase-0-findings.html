Purpose

Current Focus

Initial Coinbase spot feasibility for approximately $50 capital, approved quote-currency policy, and the economic question that now matters most: whether production-account fee burden leaves enough room for meaningful net growth.

Research Findings

F-001: Which Coinbase API product should VectorQuay target for v1 spot trading?

Question: Which Coinbase API surface is the correct primary target for automated Coinbase spot trading in v1?

Conclusion: VectorQuay v1 should target the Coinbase Advanced Trade API as the primary Coinbase trading interface.

Source: Coinbase states that “The Coinbase Advanced API lets you manage orders, products, and fees with our new v3 endpoints” and “supports advanced trading features only.” Source: What is Coinbase Advanced?

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: API design, account integration assumptions, and later implementation planning should standardize around Advanced Trade, not older Coinbase retail API patterns.

Unresolved assumptions: Need to confirm any account-specific restrictions relevant to the intended account and region.

F-002: Are spot-oriented order types available through the target API?

Question: Does the target Coinbase API support the order types needed for a v1 spot-only product?

Conclusion: Coinbase Advanced Trade documentation shows support for spot-relevant order configurations including market, limit, stop-limit, and fill-or-kill style order structures. For v1, VectorQuay should likely constrain itself to a simpler subset such as market IOC and limit GTC/GTD until feasibility and risk findings justify more complexity.

Source: Coinbase Advanced order documentation lists `market_market_ioc`, `limit_limit_gtc`, `limit_limit_gtd`, `stop_limit_stop_limit_gtc`, `stop_limit_stop_limit_gtd`, and `limit_limit_fok`. Source: Advanced API Order Management

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: The platform can likely support a basic spot execution path without needing complex order behavior on day one.

Unresolved assumptions: Need confirmation of which order subset best fits the low-capital v1 strategy and whether account/product conditions restrict availability.

F-003: Does the target API align with the approved trade-only account policy?

Question: Does Coinbase Advanced Trade API scope align with the approved v1 policy that the bot should trade only and should not automate deposits or withdrawals?

Conclusion: Yes, current Coinbase help documentation explicitly states that the Advanced API “does not duplicate core Coinbase functions for account deposits, withdrawals, and transactions” and “supports advanced trading features only.”

Source: What is Coinbase Advanced?

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: This strengthens the architectural fit between the approved trade-only policy and the chosen Coinbase API surface.

Unresolved assumptions: We still need to ensure no implementation path accidentally mixes other Coinbase account features into the product scope.

Source: Get Product and Get Public Product

Source: Get Public Product Book and Get Public Market Trades

F-006: Do current Coinbase fee rules increase risk for a low-capital strategy?

Question: Do current Coinbase Advanced fee rules create a meaningful Phase 0 risk for a strategy starting with approximately $50?

Conclusion: Yes. Coinbase Advanced uses a maker/taker fee model, fees vary by fee tier, and tiers depend on trailing USD trading volume. For a low-capital v1 product, fee drag is a first-order risk because frequent taker-style trading can materially reduce net growth at small account size.

Source: Coinbase Advanced fees

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: VectorQuay should treat fee drag as a primary feasibility constraint. The platform should not assume that high-frequency trading is economically viable at approximately $50 simply because the API supports it.

Unresolved assumptions: We still need account-specific fee-tier visibility or operator confirmation to model the likely effective fee burden more precisely.

First-Pass Screening Framework

SF-001: Proposed v1 Candidate Pair Filter

Question: What first-pass rules should VectorQuay use to approve or reject Coinbase spot pairs for v1 under the current research state?

Conclusion: Until live authenticated screening is available, VectorQuay should use a conservative candidate filter that approves only liquid USD/USDC spot pairs with normal trading status, small minimum-size requirements, and acceptable spread/market-quality metrics.

Source: Synthesized from F-004, F-005, F-006, F-008, and F-010.

Confidence: Medium to high after authenticated product inspection.

Impact on v1: This creates a defensible screening method now, even before final pair-level data is inspected live.

Unresolved assumptions: Numeric thresholds below are provisional and should be validated against live Coinbase product data.

Proposed Approval Conditions

  • Quote currency must be `USD` or `USDC`.
  • Product type must be spot-oriented and not view-only for the target account.
  • Product must not be disabled, trading-disabled, or in an abnormal restricted state.
  • `quote_min_size` and `base_min_size` must allow practical trade sizing within a roughly $50 account.
  • 24-hour volume must be high enough that the pair is not obviously illiquid relative to v1 capital size.
  • Observed spread and/or spread basis points must remain low enough that fee-plus-spread drag does not dominate expected gains.
  • Price and quantity increments must support small-capital order sizing without excessive rounding waste.

Provisional Rejection Conditions

  • Quote currency is not `USD` or `USDC`.
  • Product is disabled, trading-disabled, or not tradeable for the target account.
  • Minimum size or increment rules make meaningful position sizing unrealistic at approximately $50.
  • Spread appears too wide for a low-capital system that must overcome both spread and fees.
  • Volume or market quality appears too weak for disciplined v1 trading.
  • Pair complexity or tradability flags indicate unusual handling that would add unnecessary v1 risk.

SF-002: Proposed Initial Asset Bias

Question: What types of assets should be favored in the first live asset-screening pass?

Conclusion: The first pass should strongly favor large, liquid spot assets quoted in USD or USDC and avoid low-liquidity speculative assets until live data proves they satisfy spread, size, and market-quality thresholds.

Source: Synthesized from fee-drag risk, spread-measurement capability, and the approved project risk posture.

Confidence: Medium to high.

Impact on v1: The likely first candidate set should be major liquid assets rather than meme/speculative assets, regardless of narrative upside.

Unresolved assumptions: Exact expansion beyond the current approved-candidate and watchlist classes still requires additional later screening.

SF-003: Proposed No-Trade Rule

Question: What should VectorQuay do if no asset passes the final Phase 0 screening rules?

Conclusion: `No trade` must remain a valid and expected system outcome. If no approved asset meets the viability rules, VectorQuay should preserve capital and avoid forced trades.

Source: Consistent with the approved TRD requirement that no-trade is a valid system action.

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: The product should optimize for valid opportunity selection, not constant activity.

Unresolved assumptions: None at the policy level; only implementation details remain for later phases.

SF-004: Analysis Universe vs Live Trading Universe

Question: How should VectorQuay treat the full Coinbase-listed asset set over time without weakening current v1 controls?

Conclusion: All Coinbase-listed spot assets should be eligible for ongoing analysis and discovery in future phases, but only a dynamically screened subset should be eligible for live trading. New or newly discovered assets should enter a lifecycle such as discovered, observed, watchlist, eligible, active, or blocked rather than becoming immediately tradable.

Source: Product direction approved during Phase 0 collaboration and aligned with current feasibility findings.

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: The current narrow live universe remains intact, while future platform direction stays broad enough to discover new Coinbase listings and reassess old exclusions automatically.

Unresolved assumptions: The exact lifecycle automation and review cadence belong to later implementation phases.

SF-005: How should blockchain and mempool activity be treated in the future signal stack?

Question: Should VectorQuay implement mempool or MEV-style execution tactics for Coinbase Advanced spot trading, and should blockchain activity still be considered as a future signal source?

Conclusion: VectorQuay should not implement miner or validator fee-bidding, mempool-priority, or other MEV-style execution tactics for Coinbase Advanced spot trading because Coinbase spot uses centralized exchange matching rather than on-chain swap inclusion ordering. However, blockchain activity may still be valuable in future phases as an informational signal source, including exchange inflow and outflow monitoring, whale-transfer monitoring, unusual wallet activity, and stablecoin flow analysis.

Source: Coinbase Exchange matching-engine documentation and Ethereum gas and fee documentation, interpreted in the context of the approved Coinbase Advanced spot-only scope. Sources: Coinbase Exchange Matching Engine and Ethereum Gas and Fees.

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: This preserves blockchain intelligence as a tracked future signal-source area so it is not forgotten, while keeping Coinbase spot execution logic aligned with how the exchange actually works.

Unresolved assumptions: Future phases would still need separate research on which blockchain-derived signals are legally, technically, and economically useful enough to justify implementation.

Low-Capital Viability Model

VM-001: Proposed Viability Conditions for Approximately $50

Question: What conditions must be true for VectorQuay to be considered practically viable at approximately $50 starting capital?

Conclusion: A roughly $50 v1 is only practically viable if all of the following remain true for the candidate asset set:

  • minimum order sizes do not force trades that consume an unreasonable share of the account,
  • price and quantity increments do not create excessive rounding waste,
  • spread plus likely fee burden leaves room for net positive expected growth, though current research suggests fees are the dominant factor for liquid majors,
  • the strategy trades selectively enough that fee drag does not dominate returns,
  • the asset list remains restricted to liquid pairs where market quality is predictably strong.

Source: Synthesized from Coinbase fee guidance, product metadata availability, and spread/market-quality endpoint research.

Confidence: Medium.

Impact on v1: This makes clear that low-capital viability is conditional and narrow, not general-purpose.

Unresolved assumptions: The remaining uncertainty is strategic, not access-related: whether the confirmed fee economics are acceptable for the approved growth ambition at the current capital level.

VM-002: What Would Make Approximately $50 Non-Viable?

Conclusion: The approved v1 model should be treated as economically non-viable at approximately $50 if live research shows one or more of the following:

  • candidate products require minimum trade sizes that are too large relative to the account,
  • maker/taker fee burden plus spread leaves too little room for net growth,
  • approved assets with acceptable quality are too few to support disciplined opportunity selection,
  • practical execution would force overconcentration or overtrading.

Impact on v1: If these conditions hold, the correct Phase 0 decision may be to proceed only with a higher capital floor or narrower scope.

VM-003: Current Working Position

Conclusion: The current research does not justify rejecting the product at approximately $50 yet, but it does justify treating approximately $50 as a fragile operating point. The most defensible current position is:

  • `technically feasible` at the API and policy level,
  • `economically weak at low volume` if fee conditions resemble the temporary research account,
  • `likely only viable` with a narrow, liquid, low-turnover asset policy and strong preference for maker-friendly execution where possible.

Confidence: Medium to high.

VM-004: Refined Headline Conclusion

Conclusion: VectorQuay currently appears technically feasible and operationally coherent, but low-capital economic viability remains conditionally acceptable rather than demonstrated. The strongest current evidence indicates that, for liquid major pairs, fees are the dominant low-capital constraint and spreads are secondary.

Confidence: Medium to high.

Impact on v1: This supports continued Phase 0 work and later implementation planning, but it does not yet justify assuming that a roughly $50 account can be traded aggressively with attractive net economics.

Final Phase 0 Recommendation

PR-001: Final Recommendation

Question: Based on completed Phase 0 research, what is the most defensible recommendation?

Conclusion: `Proceed with narrower scope.` The completed Phase 0 evidence supports continuing the project, but only under a disciplined initial operating model:

  • Coinbase Advanced Trade API as the sole v1 trading surface,
  • spot only, trade only,
  • USD and USDC as the only approved quote currencies,
  • a controlled 10-asset initial candidate set biased toward liquid majors and stronger next-tier liquid spot assets,
  • low-turnover selective trading rather than high-frequency behavior,
  • strong skepticism toward taker-heavy execution at low volume,
  • `no trade` as a normal and expected outcome when no setup passes screening.

Source: Synthesized from current findings F-001 through F-014, SF-001 through SF-003, AH-001 through AH-003, VM-001 through VM-004, and AN-001.

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: The project should continue, but the early live-trading ambition should be narrowed toward liquid, low-friction, low-turnover opportunities instead of broad aggressive coverage. The current evidence supports proceeding with discipline, not proceeding with speed-at-all-costs assumptions.

Unresolved assumptions: The main remaining question is strategic and economic judgment, not missing technical visibility.

PR-002: Why Not “Proceed As Planned” Yet?

Conclusion: The current research does not justify a full “proceed as planned” recommendation because the product still lacks evidence on:

  • actual pair-level spread quality under live inspection,
  • practical screening results for candidate versus rejected assets.

PR-003: What Would Move the Recommendation?

Conclusion:

  • `Proceed as planned` would require a business decision that the currently observed fee economics are acceptable for a very narrow major-asset v1.
  • `Proceed with higher required capital` would become appropriate if the business goal remains aggressive growth despite the already confirmed Intro 1 fee burden.
  • `Stop` would only become likely if authenticated screening shows the approved v1 constraints are structurally incompatible with meaningful Coinbase spot trading.

Fee Tier Upgrade Research

FU-001: How does a Coinbase Advanced account become a “higher volume” account?

Question: What does it mean on Coinbase Advanced for an account to move into a better fee state, and is there a manual “upgrade” process?

Conclusion: The normal path is automatic volume-based tiering. Coinbase states that Advanced fee tiers update hourly based on trailing trading volume. In addition, Coinbase offers a VIP program for eligible traders outside the UK, where level is determined by trailing 30-day trading volume or asset balance and benefits include discounted trading fees. Coinbase also documents a limited status-match / fee-upgrade path for certain traders coming from other exchanges.

Source: Coinbase Advanced fees, Coinbase Advanced VIP, and Coinbase Advanced fee upgrade incentive.

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: For most users, “upgrade the account” does not mean filing a normal support request to unlock lower fees. It means increasing eligible trading volume over time, meeting VIP criteria where available, or qualifying for a status-match style incentive if applicable.

Unresolved assumptions: Need production-account region and eligibility context to know whether VIP or status match would apply.

FU-003: Practical Steps on Coinbase

Question: What steps would be required on Coinbase to move toward a better fee state?

Conclusion: The normal Coinbase path is mostly automatic rather than a manual “upgrade” workflow:

  1. Use Coinbase Advanced, not simple retail flow, for fee-tiered trading.
  2. Trade enough eligible volume that the trailing 30-day volume moves into better hourly-updated tiers.
  3. Check the Coinbase Advanced fees page while signed in to see the current tier and requirements to reach the next level.
  4. If eligible and outside the UK, review the Coinbase Advanced VIP page for automatic enrollment conditions.
  5. If there is substantial trading history on another exchange, review the Coinbase fee-upgrade incentive / VIP status-match path and submit proof if appropriate.

Source: Coinbase Advanced fees, Coinbase Advanced VIP, and Coinbase Advanced fee upgrade incentive.

Confidence: High.

Impact on v1: For the likely VectorQuay use case, the realistic path is not “manually upgrading” the account but either accepting the current retail-volume economics or materially increasing trading volume/capital over time.

Unresolved assumptions: The remaining uncertainty is whether future volume growth or program eligibility could improve the fee state enough to justify a more aggressive operating model.
